Media and Information Languages

Learning Media, Shaping Minds

Understanding how media creates meaning, how audiences interpret it, and how texts are designed, evaluated, and produced in the digital world.

What is Media Language?

Media Language refers to how producers use signs, symbols, and techniques to communicate meaning through advertisements, films, television, and digital media.

  • Denotative: Literal meaning.
  • Connotative: Implied meaning shaped by culture and experience.

Text Information & Creators

Key creators of text-based media:

  • Author
  • Writer
  • Contributor
  • Columnist
  • Blogger
  • Editor
  • Publisher

Types of Text

  • Fiction – imaginative writing.
  • Nonfiction – fact-based writing.

Text as Visual Communication

  • Serif – classic and formal.
  • Sans Serif – clean and modern.
  • Slab Serif – bold and heavy.
  • Script – elegant and decorative.
  • Decorative – artistic and thematic.

Design Principles

  • Emphasis
  • Appropriateness
  • Proximity
  • Alignment
  • Organization
  • Repetition
  • Contrast

Media Codes

Symbolic Codes

Setting, mise-en-scène, acting, and color.

Technical Codes

Camera work, editing, sound, and lighting.

Written Codes

Visible text, dialogue, and lyrics.

Types of Conventions

  • Form – expected structure of media.
  • Story – narrative structure.
  • Genre – common elements in media types.

About J&J

J&J Media Learning is an educational initiative dedicated to helping students understand how media works, how information is created, and how meaning is constructed in the digital age.

We aim to build critical thinkers who can analyze media responsibly, communicate effectively, and participate meaningfully in a connected world.

Our focus areas include media literacy, visual communication, design principles, and audience awareness — all aligned with modern school curricula.
